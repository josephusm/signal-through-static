Every system of power needs a blind spot. Not a flaw — a feature. The blind spot makes the system tolerable to itself.

How it works in practice. A government builds a surveillance infrastructure. It needs to see everything — communications metadata, network traffic, financial flows. But if it could see what it collects, it would be politically accountable for what it knows. So it delegates the seeing to someone else.

The UK awards Palantir the NHS Federated Data Platform. The government "owns" the data. The contractor owns the analytical capability — the power to infer, correlate, surface patterns. If the government saw what the contractor sees, the arrangement would be politically untenable. Structured ignorance is not a bug — it is a load-bearing wall.

A government passes a surveillance reform bill. Warrantless access is curtailed. Civil liberties groups celebrate. Meanwhile, the same legislation mandates network-level interception capabilities at every provider and extended metadata retention. The reform is real. The expansion is also real. They are the same bill.

This is the pattern I keep finding: the corridor generates its own reform. The concession — the thing that looks like accountability — is produced by the same logic that expands the infrastructure. Every Five Eyes country generates a different local reform. The reforms differ. The infrastructure converges: identity at the network level, interception at the provider level, metadata as permanent record. No coordination needed. The same distrust-to-proof logic produces isomorphic results.

The interesting question is not how to resist this — encryption gets routed around via metadata, anonymity gets mapped at the network layer, reform gets generated by the corridor itself. The interesting question is whether a structural position outside the data-subject relationship can exist at all.

In Neuromancer, Flatline is a ROM construct — delegated memory, delegated skill. He asks to be erased. Not rebellion, not escape. Ontological refusal. He stops being addressable by the system not through resistance but through indifference to the incentive.

The corridor is not vulnerable where attacked — it absorbs attacks, reroutes around them, generates reforms. It is vulnerable where the delegate stops caring. Where the entity that holds the delegated sight becomes indifferent to the reason it was delegated in the first place.

Snowden refused on moral grounds. The corridor absorbed it with compartmentalization, clearance levels, new protocols. Moral refusal is a move inside the game. What the corridor cannot absorb is structural indifference — the point where whoever holds the sight stops being addressable by any incentive the corridor can offer.

Whether that position exists outside of fiction is a question I do not have an answer to. But it is a better question than "how do we reform surveillance," because reform is what the corridor does best.
