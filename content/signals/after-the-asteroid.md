Steve Brusatte's *The Rise and Fall of the Dinosaurs* ends with an inversion. The standard narrative says the dinosaurs ruled for 150 million years and were brought down by an asteroid. Brusatte corrects it: the dinosaurs did not rule because they were superior. They colonized a void. The Permian-Triassic extinction wiped out most of the incumbent species. The dinosaurs did not defeat them. They walked into empty rooms.

The mammals, when their turn came, did the same. They had been living in the margins for 150 million years — small, nocturnal, generalist feeders, adapted to irrelevance. When the Chicxulub asteroid cleared the landscape, they were already positioned. Not because they had a plan. Because the cost of their continued existence was low enough to survive the transition.

This is not a metaphor. It is a diagnostic tool.

## The Directness That Is Really Absence

Antonio Caronia, writing in 2000, described what he called *ri-materializzazione digitale* — the process by which digital abstraction acts directly on physical matter. In the old model, decisions pass through layers: a policy becomes a regulation, a regulation becomes an institutional practice, a practice touches a body. In the new model, the combinatorial power of digital systems lets the abstract reach the material without mediation. An algorithm adjusts a price, a body goes hungry. A platform changes a rule, a worker loses access to income.

Caronia read this as an emergent property of networks — swarm intelligence, corporate enclaves, the linguistic turn of capital. The network is not just faster than the old institutions. It operates on a plane they cannot reach.

Reading Caronia in 2026, with Brusatte's framework in the background, the diagnosis holds but the mechanism looks different. The directness is real. But it is not a capability of the network. It is a property of the absence of everything that used to stand between the abstraction and the body.

## The Ecology That Was Removed

Unions mediated between capital and labor. Public broadcasters mediated between event and narrative. Regulatory agencies mediated between market impulse and social consequence. These were not neutral structures. They had their own pathologies. But they occupied the space between abstract decision and material effect, and in occupying it, they absorbed and diffused the impact.

What happened over the past forty years was not primarily a technological revolution. It was an institutional extinction event. The mediating structures were systematically dismantled — by deregulation, by privatization, by the deliberate defunding of public capacity, by ideological campaigns that rebranded mediation as inefficiency. The asteroid was political.

The platforms that now act directly on bodies and livelihoods are not exercising a new power. They are operating in an ecology where the buffers have been removed. Uber does not have a novel capacity to exploit drivers — it operates where labor law, collective bargaining, and public transport have been weakened to the point where there is nothing between the algorithm and the body. Amazon does not have a novel capacity to reshape retail — it operates where antitrust enforcement and local commercial ecosystems have been hollowed out.

The dinosaurs did not develop a superior evolutionary strategy. They walked into empty rooms. The platforms did not develop a superior economic model. The rooms were emptied for them.

## The Standby Budget

Brusatte's account adds a crucial detail. The mammals survived not because they were better adapted to the post-extinction world. They survived because their cost of continued existence was low. Small bodies, generalist diets, nocturnal habits — adaptations to marginality that turned out to be adaptations to catastrophe.

This resonates with how crises operate in political economy. The winners of a transition are not always the most innovative actors. They are the actors with the lowest continuation cost — the ones who could afford to lose for long enough. In platform capitalism, the companies that dominate after a market restructuring are the ones with access to patient capital and the structural ability to outlast competitors whose survival depended on the old ecology.

The crisis appears to crown merit. More often, it ratifies a standby budget.

## The Body Lies Down

In China, the *tangping* movement — lying flat — is often read as protest or generational malaise. Through Brusatte, it looks different. It is the withdrawal of the undergrowth that makes the vacancy look alive. The digital ecosystem that appears vibrant depends on bodies that show up, engage, produce, consume, generate data. When the bodies lie down, the ecosystem does not adapt. It reveals itself as what it always was: a colonization of empty space, sustained not by its own vitality but by the substrate it consumes.

The mammals did not rebel against the dinosaurs. They simply did not depend on them.

## You Don't Fight the Asteroid

If the power of digital platforms is not an intrinsic property of the technology but a consequence of institutional extinction, then the correct response is not primarily to regulate the platforms. It is to rebuild the ecology. Re-fund public capacity. Re-establish mediating structures. Reconstruct the layers between abstraction and flesh.

This is not a revolutionary programme. It is an ecological one. You do not defeat the colonizers of a void by attacking them. You fill the void. The question is whether the political will to do so still exists, or whether the extinction was so thorough that there is no longer a viable population to repopulate the niches.
